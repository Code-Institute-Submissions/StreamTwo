Introduction - European Union

There have been many attempts of European unification by the use of force and aggressive foreign policy. First the Romans succeed to obey vast part of the continent. Centuries later Napoleon vanquishes Europe, and during the XX century Germany conquers almost the whole continent by force. All these conquering wars and bloodshed conflicts weaken Europe and by the end of the Second World War, Europe is all in ruins and loses its leading positions on the global scale. The fast growing economies of the USA and China set Europe in a disadvantageous position. Formerly dominant European nations are now weakened in economic and military scope. The export of Europeans products and goods is seriously laboured due to the intense economy of USA, China and later on – Japan. The only solution to the specific situation is the establishment of a union, in order to prevent two main problems about the continent’s dark economic perspectives – Europe will try to be competitive to the aforementioned countries and it will try to prevent the event of a future conflict on its territory, by establishing a strong economic collaboration. At first the economics of six Western European countries are united by the European Coal and Steel Community ratified in 1951, which over time by a number of new agreements and treaties it is transformed to the European Union in 1992 by the Maastricht Treaty. Thus Europe is trying to hold on its leading position in economic and technological scope, which has held for centuries, by creating a unique of its kind economic union.
